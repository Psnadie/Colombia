The Country

A territory of depth and diversity

Colombia occupies the northwestern corner of South America — the only country on the continent bordered by both the Pacific Ocean and the Caribbean Sea. Its geography holds three ranges of the Andes, the eastern plains of the Orinoquía, the dense Amazonian rainforest, and two distinct coastlines. Each region carries its own climate, its own culture, its own pace.

Home to over fifty indigenous nations and a living Afro-Colombian heritage rooted in the Caribbean coast, Colombia's culture is not a single story. It is a layering of languages, foods, rhythms, and landscapes — each one worth moving slowly through.

The country spans 1.14 million square kilometres and a population of 51 million. Its capital, Bogotá, sits at 2,600 metres above sea level; its Caribbean beaches are near sea level; its Amazon tributaries run through equatorial rainforest. Altitude, not latitude, governs the climate.

A living history

A country that has witnessed centuries, and continues to rise

Long before the arrival of Europeans, the territory now called Colombia held some of the most complex civilisations in the Americas. The Muisca people of the Andean highlands developed sophisticated trade networks and the legend of El Dorado — the gilded man — that drew Spanish expeditions inland. The Tayrona of the Sierra Nevada, the Zenú of the Caribbean plains, and dozens of other nations each left deep imprints on the land, in language, in cosmology, in the organisation of water and stone.

Spanish colonisation began in the early sixteenth century, bringing enslaved Africans to the coastal lowlands and establishing the viceroyalty of New Granada with Bogotá as its administrative centre. This period layered European baroque architecture over indigenous settlements, producing the colonial cities that remain — Cartagena, Mompox, Popayán — as physical witnesses to three centuries of encounter.

Independence came on 20 July 1810, a date still observed as a national holiday. The nineteenth century brought political conflict; the twentieth brought transformation, growth, and difficult chapters that the country has confronted with institutional courage. Contemporary Colombia is a country of writers, scientists, musicians, engineers, and farmers. It preserves, it builds, and it continues to unfold.
